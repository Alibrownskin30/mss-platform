Methodology

MSS Protocol focuses on structural token risk. The scanner is designed to highlight authority exposure, holder concentration, hidden control patterns, liquidity fragility, whale behaviour, and risk trend movement.

What MSS is measuring

MSS is not a promise engine. It is a security intelligence layer. The scanner combines on-chain token data, holder concentration, public market context, and best-effort wallet linkage heuristics to surface signals that often matter before a token failure becomes obvious.

Authorities

  • Mint Authority: if still present, token supply may be increased.
  • Freeze Authority: if still present, transfers may be restricted or frozen.
  • MSS treats active authorities as structural risk factors because they can change token behaviour after launch.

Holder concentration

  • Top1 / Top5 / Top10 / Top20 are calculated from the largest token accounts.
  • Where possible, token accounts are resolved to owner wallets to improve concentration context.
  • Higher concentration does not always mean malicious intent, but it increases control, liquidity, and exit risk.

Hidden control

  • MSS looks for linked holder structures among major wallets.
  • Signals include shared payer fingerprints, repeated wallet linkage, and synchronized activity patterns.
  • A token can appear decentralized by count while still being controlled by a smaller coordinated group.
Hidden control is a heuristic layer. It indicates elevated likelihood of coordination or shared operator structure, not definitive attribution.

Developer activity overlap

  • MSS scores whether major wallets show overlap patterns consistent with a linked operator or developer-controlled cluster.
  • This does not name a developer wallet. It measures structure, not identity.
  • The purpose is to surface concentration that may be intentionally fragmented.

Fresh wallet risk

  • MSS estimates the proportion of recent wallets inside the analyzed holder cluster set.
  • Heavy reliance on fresh wallets can signal manufactured distribution or coordinated early positioning.
  • This signal is best used alongside hidden control and concentration, not in isolation.

Liquidity stability

  • MSS compares liquidity against FDV to estimate how fragile market structure may be.
  • Thin liquidity relative to valuation can make price action easier to distort and exits harder during stress.
  • Liquidity strength does not eliminate risk, but weak liquidity raises it.

Whale activity

  • MSS uses coordination and synchronization signals to estimate whether large-holder behaviour is normal, active, or elevated.
  • Whale signals are intended to show pressure and structure, not guaranteed intent.
  • They become more useful over repeated scans and historical collection.

Risk trend and reputation

  • MSS stores scan history to calculate short-term trend movement such as 1h, 6h, and 24h changes.
  • Momentum labels like Escalating, Rising, Cooling, and Stable come from historical movement in recorded risk snapshots.
  • The reputation layer is a compact summary of how stable or unstable the token appears over time based on MSS signals.

Market context

  • Price, liquidity, volume, and FDV come from public market sources.
  • When direct market cap is not available, MSS can derive it from price and supply context.
  • Market data is supportive context, not the only basis of MSS security scoring.

What MSS does not claim

  • MSS does not claim definitive identity attribution.
  • MSS does not guarantee that a token is safe, unsafe, or fraudulent.
  • MSS does not replace contract audits, legal review, or independent due diligence.
The scanner is designed to improve decision quality by surfacing structural risk early. It is a serious signal layer, not a certainty engine.

This methodology will continue to expand as MSS adds deeper telemetry, alert history, richer trend analysis, and future intelligence modules.
